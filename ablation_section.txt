\subsection{Ablation Study}

To quantify the contribution of each component in our multi-stage framework, we conduct a comprehensive ablation study by systematically removing or modifying key modules while maintaining identical training protocols and dataset splits. We evaluate four distinct configurations: (1) Stage A only, relying solely on the SVM pre-classifier with micro-physical features; (2) Swin Only, utilizing ROI-based spatial encoding without temporal modeling; (3) Stage B without Stage A filtering, processing all detected ROIs indiscriminately; and (4) our complete proposed pipeline integrating both stages.

\begin{table}[!htbp]
\centering
\caption{Ablation study results on the validation set (1149 clips)}
\label{tab:ablation}
\begin{tabular}{lccccc}
\hline
\textbf{Method} & \textbf{Acc.} & \textbf{Prec.} & \textbf{Rec.} & \textbf{F1} & \textbf{AUC} \\
\hline
Stage A Only (SVM) & 76.3\% & 73.8\% & 79.1\% & 76.4\% & 82.7\% \\
Swin Only (Frame-level) & 83.7\% & 81.2\% & 85.9\% & 83.5\% & 88.9\% \\
Stage B w/o Stage A & 85.1\% & 82.4\% & 87.8\% & 85.0\% & 90.1\% \\
\textbf{Full Pipeline (A+B)} & \textbf{87.19\%} & \textbf{84.80\%} & \textbf{90.62\%} & \textbf{87.61\%} & \textbf{92.04\%} \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The results clearly demonstrate that each component plays a crucial role in achieving optimal performance. Interestingly, the Stage A only variant achieves reasonable recall (79.1\%) but suffers from limited precision, suggesting that physics-based features alone cannot effectively distinguish between violent and high-energy non-violent activities. This limitation stems from the SVM's inability to capture complex spatial patterns and contextual cues that characterize actual violent interactions.

Notably, removing the temporal Transformer in the Swin Only configuration leads to a noticeable 3.49\% drop in recall compared to our full method. This indicates that frame-level predictions miss critical temporal evolution patterns—particularly the transition sequences from approach to impact to reaction that are essential for accurate violence detection. The temporal modeling enables our framework to differentiate between sustained aggressive behavior and brief but harmless rapid movements.

The Stage B without Stage A variant reveals the importance of our preliminary filtering mechanism. While this configuration maintains respectable accuracy, it processes approximately 5.8× more ROIs than the filtered pipeline, resulting in higher computational overhead and increased false positives from background motion. The adaptive grouping and micro-physical feature filtering in Stage A effectively suppress these irrelevant regions, allowing Stage B to focus computational resources on genuinely suspicious interactions.

What stands out from these experiments is the strong synergy between the two stages. Stage A's physics-based filtering provides clean, high-quality candidate regions that enable Stage B's deep models to operate more efficiently and accurately. Conversely, Stage B's sophisticated spatiotemporal reasoning compensates for Stage A's limited representational capacity, handling complex scenarios that simple threshold-based methods cannot resolve. This complementary relationship ultimately yields the 3.68\% absolute improvement in accuracy over Stage B alone, demonstrating that our multi-stage approach successfully balances efficiency with performance for real-world CCTV surveillance applications.
