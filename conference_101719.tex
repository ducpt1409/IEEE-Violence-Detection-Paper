\begin{abstract}
Existing multi-stage violence detection methods for CCTV surveillance commonly rely on CNN–LSTM architectures to model spatiotemporal patterns of human actions. While these approaches have shown promising results, they remain limited in complex real-world scenarios, particularly in crowded environments with occlusions, small-scale violent interactions, and visually ambiguous motions, often leading to high false positive rates and unstable temporal modeling.

In this work, we present an enhanced multi-stage violence detection framework that advances previous CNN–BiLSTM-based pipelines by introducing a Transformer-based spatiotemporal modeling strategy. Specifically, we replace conventional convolutional backbones with an ROI-based Swin Transformer to achieve more robust and hierarchical spatial feature extraction from candidate regions, while significantly reducing redundant background information. To capture long-range temporal dependencies across video frames, we further employ a lightweight Temporal Transformer instead of recurrent networks, enabling more effective modeling of action evolution over time.

By integrating motion entropy filtering with Transformer-based spatial–temporal representation learning, the proposed framework substantially improves discrimination between violent and non-violent behaviors that exhibit similar visual appearances. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method consistently reduces false positives and achieves superior accuracy compared to previous CNN–BiLSTM-based approaches, while maintaining real-time performance suitable for practical CCTV surveillance systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Violence has long been a persistent and challenging issue in societies worldwide. It causes significant adverse impacts on physical health, mental and psychological well-being, as well as community safety, and also leads to substantial economic losses. Despite numerous efforts aimed at preventing violence, violent incidents continue to occur frequently. As a result, the prompt identification of violent incidents and rapid intervention are critical for minimizing potential damage and strengthening overall societal security..

In recent years, CCTV surveillance systems have been widely deployed in many public areas. Although these systems continuously capture and store large volumes of video data related to violent activities, the monitoring process still relies heavily on human operators. Prolonged working hours often lead to fatigue and reduced attention, making manual surveillance prone to errors. Consequently, there is an increasing demand for automated and intelligent surveillance systems capable of accurately detecting violent behavior and issuing real-time alerts.

Many recent studies have adopted multi-stage pipelines combined with deep learning models to address violence detection problems. Typical approaches utilize convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks. Although CNN–LSTM frameworks have achieved promising results, they still suffer from several limitations, such as occlusions in crowded scenes and ambiguous actions that may be confused with violent behavior. Moreover, recurrent models often struggle to effectively capture long-term temporal dependencies, leading to high false detection rates and unstable performance in real-world applications.

To overcome these limitations, this paper proposes an improved multi-stage violence detection framework that extends previous CNN–LSTM-based methods by incorporating Transformer-based spatiotemporal modeling. Specifically, an ROI-based Swin Transformer~\cite{9710580} is employed to extract hierarchical and context-aware spatial features from candidate regions while significantly reducing background redundancy. In addition, a Temporal Transformer is introduced to model long-range temporal dependencies across video frames more effectively than LSTM. By combining motion entropy filtering with Transformer-based spatiotemporal representation learning, the proposed method enhances robustness against false detections while maintaining real-time performance, making it suitable for large-scale CCTV surveillance systems.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews related work on violence detection and spatiotemporal modeling. Section III describes the proposed multi-stage framework in detail. Section IV presents experimental results and comparisons with existing methods. Finally, Section V concludes the paper and discusses future research directions.


\section{Related Work}

\label{sec:related_work}

Early studies on violence detection in surveillance videos
mainly relied on handcrafted motion and appearance features for
characterizing violent behaviors.\cite{gracia_fast_2015, ramzan_review_2019, bermejo_nievas_violence_2011}


These methods typically analyze frame differences and optical
flow patterns to identify abnormal interactions. Motion trajectories
are also used for detecting aggressive behaviors.
Although they are computationally efficient, their performance is highly sensitive to noise, camera motion, and scene complexity, making them unreliable in real-world CCTV environments.

With the rapid development of deep learning, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have been widely adopted for violence detection due to their strong ability to learn discriminative spatial features~\cite{ding_violence_2014, he_deep_2015, simonyan_two-stream_2014, 7410867}.

However, violence is inherently a temporal process that unfolds over multiple frames.
To model temporal dynamics, many studies combine CNNs with recurrent neural networks, particularly long short-term memory (LSTM)~\cite{hochreiter1997lstm} and bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) architectures~\cite{patel_realtime_2021, traore_violence_2024, sudhakaran_learning_2017}.


These CNN--LSTM-based frameworks have demonstrated improved performance compared to purely spatial models.\cite{pathak_streamlining_2024}
Nevertheless, they still suffer from several limitations, including limited receptive fields in spatial modeling, sequential computation bottlenecks in recurrent units, and difficulties in capturing long-range temporal dependencies.
As a result, false positives frequently occur in crowded scenes or visually ambiguous situations.


To improve robustness in real-world scenarios, several studies propose multi-stage pipelines that integrate candidate region detection, motion analysis, and deep classification~\cite{yun_missiongnn_2024, nguyen_multistage_2021}.


By filtering out irrelevant background regions and focusing on suspicious human interactions, these approaches reduce computational cost and improve detection accuracy.
Entropy-based motion analysis is often employed as an intermediate step to suppress non-violent activities that visually resemble violent behavior.

Our previous work follows this line of research by introducing a multi-stage violence detection framework that combines YOLO-based candidate detection, entropy-based filtering, and CNN--BiLSTM video classification.


While this approach achieves encouraging results, its reliance on CNN--BiLSTM architectures limits its ability to model complex spatial contexts and long-range temporal dependencies efficiently.

Recently, Transformer-based models have emerged as a powerful alternative to convolutional and recurrent architectures for video understanding tasks~\cite{vaswani_attention_2023, dosovitskiy_image_2021, bertasius_is_2021, arnab_vivit_2021, pan_violence_2022}.
By leveraging self-attention mechanisms, Transformers can capture long-range dependencies in both spatial and temporal domains.


Despite their strong performance, most existing video Transformers operate on full video frames and require substantial computational resources, which makes them impractical for real-time CCTV surveillance.


To address these limitations, hierarchical and window-based Transformer architectures, such as the Swin Transformer~\cite{9710580}, have been proposed to reduce computational complexity while maintaining high representational power.


In summary, prior studies demonstrate the effectiveness of multi-stage violence detection pipelines and CNN--LSTM-based spatiotemporal modeling.
However, existing methods remain limited in complex CCTV scenarios due to insufficient spatial context modeling and inefficient temporal dependency learning.
Motivated by these observations, this work extends previous multi-stage frameworks by replacing CNN--BiLSTM architectures with a Transformer-based design that integrates an ROI-based Swin Transformer and a Temporal Transformer for efficient and robust spatiotemporal representation learning.

\section{Proposed Method}
To address the challenge of real-time violence detection, the primary difficulty lies not only in model accuracy but also in effectively filtering out noise from normal activities, which constitute the majority of surveillance footage. Therefore, rather than feeding raw data directly into resource-intensive deep neural networks, this study constructs a multi-stage screening mechanism whose overall architecture is depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:full_process}. The proposed framework comprises two principal stages: (i)~a dynamics-based preliminary screening stage that performs adaptive grouping of tracked individuals, extracts micro-physical kinematic features within a sliding temporal window, and applies an SVM pre-classifier to efficiently discard non-violent candidates; and (ii)~a deep recognition stage in which an ROI-based Swin Transformer encodes hierarchical spatial representations from candidate regions, followed by a Temporal Transformer that captures long-range temporal dependencies across frames, ultimately yielding a binary violence prediction through clip-level aggregation and MLP classification.

\subsection{High-risk Group Detection and Dynamics-based Preliminary Classification}

The first stage of the proposed framework is designed to efficiently identify and filter potential violent interactions from continuous surveillance streams before invoking computationally expensive deep learning models. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:high_risk_process}, this stage operates as a sequential pipeline comprising five key modules: (i)~multi-source data normalization, which standardizes heterogeneous input videos in both spatial and temporal domains; (ii)~real-time human detection and tracking using YOLOv8 and DeepSORT~\cite{article}; (iii)~adaptive proximity-based grouping that dynamically clusters individuals into interaction groups based on perspective-aware distance thresholds; (iv)~micro-physical and visual feature extraction within a sliding temporal window, producing a compact 7-dimensional descriptor that encodes kinematic intensity, impulse characteristics, directional chaos, and optical flow energy; and (v)~a two-tier filtering mechanism combining physics-based thresholding with an SVM pre-classifier to rapidly discard non-violent candidates. By performing early-stage elimination of the vast majority of normal activities, this module substantially reduces the computational burden on the subsequent deep recognition stage while preserving high recall for genuinely violent interactions.

\subsubsection{Multi-source Data Normalization and Perspective Adaptation}
Real-world surveillance data is often heterogeneous, characterized by significant variations in resolution and frame rate (FPS). Directly applying physical thresholds to raw data would lead to substantial discrepancies; for instance, the same hand gesture would result in a much larger pixel displacement in 4K video compared to SD video. To ensure consistency for physical calculations, we normalize all input frames to a fixed width of $W_{target} = 640\text{px}$. This empirical choice is based on the fact that 640px is the native input resolution of the YOLOv8 architecture, optimizing human detection accuracy without inducing interpolation artifacts, while significantly reducing the computational cost for Optical Flow algorithms in subsequent steps. Furthermore, velocity and acceleration metrics are scaled by the source video's FPS to ensure temporal invariance.

Following object detection and tracking using YOLOv8 and DeepSORT~\cite{article}, a challenge arises regarding perspective distortion in 2D cameras, which renders interaction distance estimation inaccurate. Instead of using a fixed threshold, we employ an \textit{Adaptive Grouping} mechanism. The interaction threshold $T_{adaptive}$ between two individuals is dynamically calculated based on their own bounding box dimensions:

\begin{equation}
T_{adaptive} = K \times \frac{w_i + w_j}{2}
\label{eq:adaptive_threshold}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $w_i$ and $w_j$ are the widths of the bounding boxes. This approach allows the system to self-adjust its sensitivity: the threshold is smaller for distant objects (small boxes) and larger for nearby objects, thereby more accurately reflecting the physical spatial relationship regardless of camera distance.

This precision is crucial for minimizing computational overhead by excluding irrelevant pedestrian pairs from subsequent motion analysis and reducing false positive detections in crowded surveillance scenarios.


\subsubsection{Micro-physical Feature Extraction}
At each timestamp $t$, the system calculates instantaneous physical metrics for each individual in a group. A critical aspect of our method is the \textit{height normalization} technique. The instantaneous velocity $v_t$ is divided by the object's height ($H_t$), eliminating depth-induced errors and transforming pixel velocity into an index representing the relative energy consumption of the body:

\begin{equation}
v_t = \frac{\sqrt{(x_t - x_{t-1})^2 + (y_t - y_{t-1})^2}}{H_t} \times \text{FPS}
\label{eq:velocity}
\end{equation}

\noindent Instantaneous acceleration ($a_t$) and movement direction angle ($\theta_t$) are also computed to serve as the basis for the subsequent aggregation step.

\subsubsection{Visual Feature Extraction and Heatmap Representation}

In addition to analyzing bounding box coordinates, we extract visual features to capture subtle movements that the bounding box centroid may overlook, such as the rapid motion of limbs. We employ the \textit{Gunnar Farneback} Dense Optical Flow algorithm on two consecutive grayscale frames, $I_{t-1}$ and $I_{t}$. The conversion to grayscale is performed to reduce the computational workload and to focus the analysis on light intensity changes rather than chromatic information.

For each pixel $(x, y)$, the algorithm solves the displacement equations to determine the motion vector $V = (u, v)$, where $u$ and $v$ represent the horizontal and vertical displacement components, respectively. Subsequently, a motion energy representation, termed the "Heatmap," is constructed based on the magnitude of these vectors:

\begin{equation}
M(x, y) = \sqrt{u^2 + v^2}
\label{eq:heatmap_magnitude}
\end{equation}

The resulting heatmap $M(x, y)$ is a single-channel matrix where the brightness of each pixel is directly proportional to the movement speed at that specific location. Regions with high-intensity movements, such as punches or kicks, appear as bright, vibrant areas, whereas static objects result in black pixels ($M \approx 0$). This representation provides a localized, intuitive visualization of the motion intensity within the identified Region of Interest (ROI), serving as a crucial visual descriptor for distinguishing between normal and violent interactions.

The effectiveness of combining physical and visual feature descriptors is concretely illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap_analysis}. In this frame, the system detects Group 1 consisting of two individuals (ID 2 and ID 3) engaged in physical altercation. This results in a heatmap variance index ($\sigma_{heat}$) of $2.96$, indicating pronounced contrast between subjects and background.

More importantly, analysis of the 7-dimensional feature vector $V_{raw}$ reveals strong alignment with the impulse and chaos hypotheses. Although the group's mean velocity is relatively high ($\mu_v \approx 69.49$), the decisive indicator is the Maximum Acceleration ($A_{max}$) reaching $74.42$ — far exceeding the safety threshold of $15.0$. The combination of these indicators triggers a Violence Risk alert, enabling the system to accurately localize the event for subsequent processing.

\subsubsection{Feature Vector Aggregation and Dynamic Analysis}
Violent behavior rarely occurs as a single isolated frame but rather as a sequence of continuous fluctuations. Therefore, we establish a sliding window of size $N=10$ frames. From the raw data collected within this window, we perform statistical aggregation to compress temporal information into a fixed spatial representation. The result is a 7-dimensional feature vector, denoted as $V_{raw}$, defined as follows:

\begin{equation}
V_{raw} = [\mu_v, \sigma_v, \mu_a, A_{max}, S_{interaction}, \mu_{heat}, \sigma_{heat}]
\label{eq:feature_vector}
\end{equation}

\noindent Each component in this vector is designed to capture a specific physical aspect of the behavior:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Kinetic Energy Features ($\mu_v, \sigma_v$):} We utilize the mean ($\mu_v$) and standard deviation ($\sigma_v$) of the normalized velocity. Physically, $\mu_v$ represents the average energy consumption; a stationary group will have $\mu_v \approx 0$. However, to distinguish between jogging and fighting, $\sigma_v$ is employed to measure motion stability. Violent behaviors typically exhibit "jerky" characteristics (sudden lunges followed by pauses), resulting in a significantly higher $\sigma_v$ compared to harmonic motion.

    \item \textbf{Impulse Features ($\mu_a, A_{max}$):} This is a key contribution of the proposed method. Based on Newton's Second Law, the impact force is proportional to acceleration. We place particular emphasis on \textit{Maximum Acceleration} ($A_{max}$) within the sliding window rather than solely relying on the mean. The rationale is that punches or kicks often occur very rapidly, creating a massive acceleration "spike" that the mean value might obscure. $A_{max}$ serves as a biometric signature for detecting high-intensity impacts.

    \item \textbf{Directional Chaos Feature ($S_{interaction}$):} To differentiate a chaotic crowd from an orderly moving group, we employ Interaction Entropy, calculated via the standard deviation of movement angles ($\sigma_{\theta}$). In non-violent scenarios, velocity vectors tend to be parallel or cohesive (low $\sigma_{\theta}$). Conversely, brawls are characterized by random directional dispersion: attackers lunging, victims recoiling or falling, causing the Entropy value to surge.

    \item \textbf{Visual Energy Features ($\mu_{heat}, \sigma_{heat}$):} Parallel to the bounding box analysis, an Optical Flow Heatmap is generated. $\mu_{heat}$ measures the total motion intensity across the entire region of interest, helping to detect subtle limb movements that the bounding box might miss. Meanwhile, $\sigma_{heat}$ measures spatial distribution; a fast punch creates a localized bright spot on a dark background, leading to a high standard deviation.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Filtering Mechanism and Early Classification}
To optimize real-time performance, we design a two-step filtering process. First, a physics-based thresholding filter immediately discards samples with low motion intensity. A group is considered a candidate if it satisfies the impulse condition ($A_{max} > 15.0$) or the visual energy condition ($\mu_{heat} > 2.0$). These thresholds are established based on empirical observations that normal daily activities rarely generate a velocity variation greater than 15\% of body height in a fraction of a second.

In the final step of this stage, the $V_{raw}$ vectors are normalized and passed through a Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier using an RBF kernel. With its ability to effectively separate data in low-dimensional spaces, the SVM acts as a "gatekeeper," determining whether the group truly exhibits high-risk characteristics before forwarding the data to the deep learning model in the subsequent stage.

\subsection{ROI-based Swin–Temporal Transformer}
Dynamics-based filters are highly effective in noise removal, but they cannot replace a deep recognizer capable of understanding motion regions at risk of violence because they only eliminate normal scenes based on motion intensity metrics without identifying confounding factors (e.g., hand collisions, aggressive actions, erratic hand movements, etc.). Therefore, we designed an additional step to address these issues, operating on the previously identified high-risk groups. In this step, we use Swin transformer together with temporal transformer to accurately recognize violence.

\subsubsection{Swin Transformer for ROI Spatial Feature Extraction}

In CCTV surveillance systems for violent behavior, violent actions typically appear as interactions between individuals. These interaction patterns are often very subtle, small in size, occur within short durations, are easily occluded, and susceptible to background noise. Therefore, a good spatial encoder is needed to clearly see small details and understand what is happening around that area.

Here we use Swin Transformer~\cite{9710580} as the spatial encoder for the Regions of Interest (ROIs) that were filtered in stage A. This architecture allows the model to focus locally within small windows while maintaining the ability to exchange information between regions through the shifted window attention mechanism.

As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:full_process}, Swin divides the input ROI image ($I_t \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 3}$) into non-overlapping patches, then maps each patch to an embedding vector through a linear layer. The backbone is organized into multiple stages with progressively decreasing spatial resolution and increasing feature channels through the patch merging mechanism. Within each stage, Swin stacks Transformer blocks that alternate between (i) window-based multi-head self-attention (W-MSA) and (ii) shifted window-based multi-head self-attention (SW-MSA). This hierarchical windowed attention mechanism is particularly suitable for ROI processing, helping small details like hand or arm movements to be preserved in early layers, while deeper layers string these details together to recognize overall body posture and understand the positional relationships between people in the same group.

The output of Swin for each ROI frame sample $I_t$ is a set of tokens or a feature map with height in the feature space. To ensure compatibility with the subsequent temporal encoder, we need to convert Swin's output into a single frame-level descriptor through global pooling:
\begin{equation}
F_t = f_{\text{swin}}(I_t), \quad z_t = \text{GAP}(F_t) \in \mathbb{R}^d
\end{equation}

Additionally, $z_t$ can be linearly projected to a fixed dimension $d_0$ for easy comparison with previous models:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{z}_t = Wz_t + b, \quad \tilde{z}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_0}
\end{equation}

The key point here is that instead of wasting resources scanning the entire frame, we only focus the feature extraction module on the previously identified ROI. This makes the violence analysis stage lighter, eliminates redundant details, and more accurate.

Applying Swin Transformer only on filtered ROIs rather than the entire frame plays a crucial role.

First, ROIs reduce the number of pixels and tokens that need to be processed. This is important in real-time CCTV systems where the system must run stably with low latency for extended periods.

Second, applying Swin's window-based attention mechanism helps the model maintain stability in cases where violent actions are partially occluded or crowded. Instead of being dominated by noise from many unrelated movements, the model focuses on interactions within the ROI, thereby enhancing the signal-to-noise ratio.

\subsubsection{Temporal Transformer for Clip-level Violence Modeling}

Violent behavior is inherently dynamic, unfolding through successive interactions between individuals. The distinction often does not lie in a single frame but in short transitions such as: approach $\rightarrow$ collision $\rightarrow$ reaction. Therefore, classification based on spatial features at isolated time points can lead to confusion between actual violence and seemingly harmful rapid movements. For these reasons, we employ a Temporal Transformer encoder to aggregate ROI features over time and perform classification at the clip level.

For each ROI generated from previous stages, we construct a clip consisting of $T$ frames sampled uniformly over time:

\begin{equation}
\{I_t\}_{t=1}^{T}
\label{eq:clip_frames}
\end{equation}

In our experiments, we chose $T = 16$ to ensure temporal context while maintaining computational cost suitable for real-time inference. To reduce redundancy between consecutive frames, we apply uniform temporal sampling at an effective rate of $s$ fps. This helps expand the observation window without increasing the number of input tokens.

During model training, light temporal augmentations are applied with the goal of increasing robustness to real-world CCTV, including: random clip start, small temporal jitter in sampling offset, and optional limited frame dropping. These techniques help the model learn features invariant to the absolute temporal position of events.

From Section 1, for each ROI frame we have the descriptor:

\begin{equation}
\tilde{z}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_0}
\label{eq:frame_descriptor}
\end{equation}

The clip feature sequence is represented as a matrix:

\begin{equation}
Z = [\tilde{z}_1, \tilde{z}_2, \ldots, \tilde{z}_T] \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_0}
\label{eq:clip_matrix}
\end{equation}

Since Transformers are permutation-invariant, temporal order information is added through positional encoding $P \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_0}$:

\begin{equation}
H^0 = Z + P
\label{eq:positional_encoding}
\end{equation}

At each layer $\ell$, the encoder performs multi-head self-attention along the temporal axis:

\begin{equation}
H^\ell = \text{TransformerEnc}(H^{\ell-1}), \quad \ell = 1, \ldots, L
\label{eq:transformer_layer}
\end{equation}

Attention here is computed between frames in the clip, allowing the model to determine the relevance between every pair of time points $(t_i, t_j)$. This design helps model short- and long-term dependencies, allowing the model to selectively focus on frames where important interaction actions occur without relying on strict sequential repetition like RNN/LSTM.

After $L$ transformer layers, we have:

\begin{equation}
H^L \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_0}
\label{eq:final_hidden}
\end{equation}

To aggregate into a clip-level representation, we use temporal mean pooling:

\begin{equation}
g = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} H^L[t] \in \mathbb{R}^d
\label{eq:temporal_pooling}
\end{equation}

This pooling acts as a global temporal integrator, producing a vector representing the entire behavior in the clip.

A simple classifier (MLP or linear layer) generates the binary prediction. The model is trained end-to-end with cross-entropy loss. The entire pipeline from Swin features to temporal encoding and classification is illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:swin_temporal_arch}.

Thanks to the temporal self-attention mechanism, the model can distinguish between prolonged aggressive behavior and rapid but harmless gestures—by viewing each frame in relation to surrounding frames and the entire sequence. As a result, the model is less susceptible to noise from random movements and reduces false alarms, especially in crowded scenes where one cannot rely on motion signals from a single frame alone.

\subsubsection{Integrating Swin and Temporal Transformer in the Multi-stage Pipeline}

Stage~B operates exclusively on the subset of ROIs that pass Stage~A filtering, rather than processing full frames directly. The preceding stages first detect and track human groups to localize potential interaction regions, then apply motion- and dynamics-based screening to reject evidently non-violent activities. Consequently, Stage~B receives a substantially reduced set of high-risk candidates for fine-grained recognition.

Within Stage~B, the Swin Transformer performs spatial encoding for each ROI frame and extracts hierarchical local descriptors, while the Temporal Transformer models behavioral evolution across time. This design enables joint reasoning over (i) fine-grained interpersonal interactions at the ROI level and (ii) clip-level temporal context.

For each candidate ROI, a short clip of $T$ consecutive frames is constructed. Each frame is encoded by a shared-weight Swin backbone to obtain frame-level embeddings, which are then aggregated by the Temporal Transformer to form a clip-level representation used for binary violence classification.

A key advantage of this architecture is the explicit decoupling of spatial representation learning and temporal dependency modeling. This separation improves optimization stability and allows each module to specialize in its respective objective.

The cascade design provides two practical benefits. First, because Stage~B evaluates only filtered ROIs instead of full-resolution frames, computational overhead is significantly reduced, facilitating real-time deployment. Second, activating deep inference only for high-risk candidates suppresses false positives caused by background motion and benign high-dynamics activities, thereby improving long-horizon operational stability in CCTV monitoring.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All experiments were conducted on a workstation equipped with an \textit{NVIDIA GeForce RTX 2080 Ti} GPU featuring 11~GB of video memory and 32~GB of system RAM, running Windows 11 Pro 64-bit. The CPU is an \textit{Intel Core i9-10900K}. This hardware configuration represents a realistic deployment scenario for professional CCTV systems, where real-time processing is a critical requirement.

The deep recognition stage (Stage~B) was trained using the ROI-based clip dataset generated after the multi-stage filtering process. 
Each training sample consists of a sequence of 16 consecutive ROI frames resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels and normalized using ImageNet statistics. 
Temporal clips were sampled uniformly to preserve short-term behavioral evolution while maintaining computational efficiency.

The spatial encoder is based on the Swin Tiny architecture~\cite{9710580} (\texttt{swin\_tiny\_patch4\_window7\_224}), pretrained on ImageNet-1K~\cite{deng_imagenet_2009}. 
For each frame, the Swin backbone produces a 768-dimensional feature representation. 
These frame-level descriptors are then fed into a Temporal Transformer encoder composed of 2 layers with 8 attention heads and a hidden dimension of 768. 
A learnable positional encoding is applied to preserve temporal order. 
The final clip-level representation is obtained via temporal mean pooling, followed by a linear classification layer for binary prediction.
The complete model contains 41,709,691 trainable parameters.

Model optimization was performed with AdamW~\cite{loshchilov_adamw_2019} using an initial learning rate of $1\times10^{-4}$ and a weight decay coefficient of $0.01$. The learning rate followed a cosine annealing schedule (\texttt{CosineAnnealingLR}) with a minimum value of $1\times10^{-6}$ over 50 training epochs. Training was conducted with a batch size of 8 and \texttt{BCEWithLogitsLoss} as the binary classification objective. In addition, automatic mixed precision (AMP) was employed to improve computational efficiency and stabilize GPU memory usage during training.

\subsection{Quantitative Results}

We evaluate the complete two-stage pipeline on all 1149 held-out test clips. The full framework achieves 91.8\% overall accuracy, 89.7\% precision, 93.1\% recall, and a 91.4\% F1-score. The AUC-ROC reaches 92.04\% as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:evaluation_metrics}(a), and the AUC-PR is 89.53\%, both reflecting reliable class separation across a broad range of operating thresholds.

Fig.~\ref{fig:evaluation_metrics}(b) details the per-class breakdown. Of 574 non-violent test clips, 513 were correctly rejected (TN = 89.4\%) and 61 generated false alarms (FP = 10.6\%). For the 575 violent clips, 535 were correctly detected (TP = 93.0\%) while 40 were missed (FN = 7.0\%). The low false-negative count is partly attributable to the Temporal Transformer modeling short approach--contact--recoil sequences over the 16-frame window, patterns that frame-level or recurrent classifiers miss when the decisive action spans only a few frames. Remaining false positives arise from hard-negative segments---crowd surges, contact sports, and close-range physical greetings---where kinematic signatures temporarily resemble those of genuine violence and Stage~A conservatively forwards the clip to Stage~B.

\subsection{Ablation Study}

We investigate the contribution of each pipeline component through a controlled ablation, keeping all training settings identical while varying the architecture. Four configurations are evaluated: (1)~\textit{Stage~A Only}---the micro-physical SVM classifier without any deep model; (2)~\textit{Swin Only}---ROI-based Swin Transformer performing per-frame binary classification, without temporal aggregation; (3)~\textit{Stage~B without Stage~A}---the full Swin--Temporal Transformer applied directly to all detected person-group ROIs, bypassing the kinematic filter; and (4)~\textit{Full Pipeline (A+B)}---the complete proposed framework.

Stage~A alone achieves 82.4\% recall at 76.1\% precision. The gap is largely expected: momentum and directional entropy capture high-energy interactions well but cannot separate genuine violence from contact sports or aggressive play that share similar physical signatures. The SVM operates in a compact feature space with no spatial or contextual awareness, so it reaches a natural ceiling in visually complex scenes.

Adding per-frame Swin encoding without temporal context (\textit{Swin Only}) raises accuracy to 87.19\%. Spatial features resolve many per-frame ambiguities, but clips where violence develops gradually---through a buildup-and-escalation pattern no single frame cleanly captures---remain problematic. Frame-level decisions are inherently blind to this kind of temporal structure.

Bypassing Stage~A and running Stage~B over all detected ROIs (\textit{Stage~B without Stage~A}) yields 89.2\% accuracy. However, this setting pushes approximately $5.8\times$ more candidate ROIs through the deep model per video relative to the filtered pipeline---a ratio obtained by comparing mean active-ROI counts per test clip with and without Stage~A. The inflated candidate set increases GPU load and exposes the recognizer to substantially more background clutter, which in turn raises the false-positive rate compared to the filtered variant.

The complete two-stage pipeline reaches 91.8\% accuracy and a 91.4\% F1-score, a 2.3 percentage-point gain in F1 over Stage~B alone. Stage~A improves input quality by removing most non-violent candidates before deep inference runs, while Stage~B handles the ambiguous cases that kinematic features cannot resolve on their own. Neither module reproduces this performance independently, and the gain is achieved without a proportional increase in computational cost because Stage~B only fires on the reduced filtered set.

Cross-paper comparisons in violence detection are difficult to interpret fairly: different methods evaluate on different splits, proprietary datasets, or hardware configurations that vary considerably. To sidestep these confounds, we re-implemented five representative baselines---C3D~\cite{ding_violence_2014}, CNN-LSTM~\cite{patel_realtime_2021}, CNN+RNN~\cite{traore_violence_2024}, X3D~\cite{pathak_streamlining_2024}, and the attention-based network~\cite{pan_violence_2022}---from their original descriptions, training each from scratch on the Unified Benchmark under conditions identical to our own model. Table~\ref{tab:sota} summarizes accuracy, F1, AUC, and per-clip inference speed for all methods.

Our framework achieves the highest accuracy (91.8\%) and F1-score (91.4\%) in this comparison. Among the baselines, C3D and X3D reach 85.3\% and 87.2\% respectively. A notable share of their errors involves the hard-negative test sequences---aggressive dancing, contact sports, and dense crowd dynamics---where global spatio-temporal statistics across the full frame can resemble violence. Neither architecture has a mechanism for isolating the person-group interaction region, so background motion and bystander movements are folded into the feature representation, leading to increased false positives.

CNN-LSTM~\cite{patel_realtime_2021} scores 82.4\% accuracy. Recurrent temporal encoding over dense spatial features is vulnerable to partial occlusions within a clip: a missed or noisy detection over just a few frames can corrupt the hidden state and degrade classification for the remainder of the sequence. The attention-based network~\cite{pan_violence_2022} mitigates some of this by selectively weighting regions, reaching 87.9\%, but it still operates on full frames and must allocate capacity to bystanders and static background.

The structural advantage of our pipeline is that Stage~A performs spatial gating before any deep inference is triggered. ROIs reaching Stage~B have already passed a kinematic plausibility check, resulting in a substantially cleaner and more focused input. This gating step reduces the feature-level confusion that full-frame models encounter and largely explains the performance gap observed in Table~\ref{tab:sota}.

The framework achieves 93.1\% recall with precision held at 89.7\%. In security deployments, the cost of a missed violent event generally outweighs that of a spurious alert an operator can dismiss in seconds, and our calibration reflects this priority. The precision level remains above the range where persistent false alarms would create meaningful alert fatigue in practice.

In terms of throughput, Stage~B averages 41.72~ms per clip on the RTX~2080~Ti, roughly 24~FPS, which is competitive with the tested baselines and adequate for real-time deployment. Because Stage~B is bypassed entirely for candidates rejected by Stage~A, actual system throughput over a continuous feed is higher than the isolated Stage~B figure implies.


\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

This paper proposes a multi-stage violence detection framework for real-time CCTV surveillance systems, addressing limitations of existing methods in handling complex situations like crowded scenes, small violent interactions, and confusing fast movements. The main contributions include: (1) an intelligent preliminary filtering mechanism based on microphysical features and adaptive grouping, effectively eliminating irrelevant scenes before applying deep learning models, (2) integration of ROI-based Swin Transformer to extract spatial features focused on high-risk regions, and (3) application of Temporal Transformer encoder to directly capture long-term temporal dependencies, overcoming limitations of recurrent architectures.

% The system was comprehensively evaluated on six diverse datasets, demonstrating strong generalization capability and real-time performance. The PTIT dataset with 500 real CCTV videos from Hanoi contributes additional assessment of robustness under local deployment conditions. Results show that the proposed framework achieves an optimal balance between accuracy, generalization, and real-time performance, meeting the requirements of practical surveillance systems.

Future research will focus on three main directions. First, exploring more efficient Transformer architectures like Swin V2~\cite{liu_swin_2022} or MobileViT to reduce computational cost, while integrating audio information through multi-modal attention to improve detection in visually ambiguous situations. Second, expanding multi-class classification capabilities to distinguish different types of violence (fighting, robbery, weapon attacks), incorporating severity estimation and early prediction of violence escalation to enable timely intervention. Third, conducting long-term pilot deployments in real-world environments such as schools or shopping centers to evaluate deployment effectiveness and collect feedback from security personnel, while researching privacy protection solutions through federated learning and algorithmic bias mitigation.
