\begin{abstract}
Existing multi-stage violence detection methods for CCTV surveillance commonly rely on CNN–LSTM architectures to model spatiotemporal patterns of human actions. While these approaches have shown promising results, they remain limited in complex real-world scenarios, particularly in crowded environments with occlusions, small-scale violent interactions, and visually ambiguous motions, often leading to high false positive rates and unstable temporal modeling.

In this work, we present an enhanced multi-stage violence detection framework that advances previous CNN–BiLSTM-based pipelines by introducing a Transformer-based spatiotemporal modeling strategy. Specifically, we replace conventional convolutional backbones with an ROI-based Swin Transformer to achieve more robust and hierarchical spatial feature extraction from candidate regions, while significantly reducing redundant background information. To capture long-range temporal dependencies across video frames, we further employ a lightweight Temporal Transformer instead of recurrent networks, enabling more effective modeling of action evolution over time.

By integrating motion entropy filtering with Transformer-based spatial–temporal representation learning, the proposed framework substantially improves discrimination between violent and non-violent behaviors that exhibit similar visual appearances. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method consistently reduces false positives and achieves superior accuracy compared to previous CNN–BiLSTM-based approaches, while maintaining real-time performance suitable for practical CCTV surveillance systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Violence has long been a persistent and challenging issue in societies worldwide. It causes significant adverse impacts on physical health, mental and psychological well-being, as well as community safety, and also leads to substantial economic losses. Despite numerous efforts aimed at preventing violence, violent incidents continue to occur frequently. As a result, the prompt identification of violent incidents and rapid intervention are critical for minimizing potential damage and strengthening overall societal security.

In recent years, CCTV surveillance systems have been widely deployed in many public areas. Although these systems continuously capture and store large volumes of video data related to violent activities, the monitoring process still relies heavily on human operators. Prolonged working hours often lead to fatigue and reduced attention, making manual surveillance prone to errors. Consequently, there is an increasing demand for automated and intelligent surveillance systems capable of accurately detecting violent behavior and issuing real-time alerts.

Many recent studies have adopted multi-stage pipelines combined with deep learning models to address violence detection problems. Typical approaches utilize convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks. Although CNN–LSTM frameworks have achieved promising results, they still suffer from several limitations, such as occlusions in crowded scenes and ambiguous actions that may be confused with violent behavior. Moreover, recurrent models often struggle to effectively capture long-term temporal dependencies, leading to high false detection rates and unstable performance in real-world applications.

To overcome these limitations, this paper proposes an improved multi-stage violence detection framework that extends previous CNN–LSTM-based methods by incorporating Transformer-based spatiotemporal modeling. Specifically, an ROI-based Swin Transformer~\cite{9710580} is employed to extract hierarchical and context-aware spatial features from candidate regions while significantly reducing background redundancy. In addition, a Temporal Transformer is introduced to model long-range temporal dependencies across video frames more effectively than LSTM. By combining motion entropy filtering with Transformer-based spatiotemporal representation learning, the proposed method enhances robustness against false detections while maintaining real-time performance, making it suitable for large-scale CCTV surveillance systems.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews related work on violence detection and spatiotemporal modeling. Section III describes the proposed multi-stage framework in detail. Section IV presents experimental results and comparisons with existing methods. Finally, Section V concludes the paper and discusses future research directions.


\section{Related Work}

\label{sec:related_work}

Early studies on violence detection in surveillance videos primarily relied on hand-crafted motion and appearance descriptors to characterize aggressive behaviors~\cite{gracia_fast_2015, ramzan_review_2019, bermejo_nievas_violence_2011}. Representative approaches exploited frame-differencing, spatio-temporal interest points, and optical flow histograms to identify abnormal interactions, while motion trajectory analysis served as an auxiliary cue for flagging physically implausible movements. Although computationally lightweight, these methods are highly sensitive to noise, illumination variation, and camera motion, rendering them unreliable in uncontrolled CCTV environments.

With the rise of deep learning, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) became the dominant feature extractor for appearance-based violence recognition owing to their superior capacity for discriminative spatial representation~\cite{ding_violence_2014, he_deep_2015, simonyan_two-stream_2014, 7410867}. In particular, two-stream architectures~\cite{simonyan_two-stream_2014} demonstrated that fusing appearance and dense optical flow streams substantially improves classification accuracy relative to spatial-only models. Nevertheless, CNNs process frames independently and thus fail to model the sequential nature of violent events, which typically span multiple frames.

To capture temporal dynamics, a large body of subsequent work combined CNN spatial encoders with recurrent architectures, particularly long short-term memory (LSTM)~\cite{hochreiter1997lstm} networks and their bidirectional extensions~\cite{patel_realtime_2021, traore_violence_2024, sudhakaran_learning_2017}. These CNN--LSTM pipelines demonstrated measurable accuracy gains over purely spatial baselines~\cite{pathak_streamlining_2024}, yet they remain susceptible to occlusion artifacts, exhibit limited spatial receptive fields, and encounter computational bottlenecks arising from sequential hidden-state propagation. More critically, LSTM units tend to lose sensitivity to distant temporal dependencies when clip lengths exceed a few dozen frames, causing elevated false-positive rates in scenes with sustained crowd dynamics.

A parallel line of research has investigated skeleton- and pose-based representations as a complement to appearance features. By estimating body keypoints with tools such as OpenPose~\cite{cao_openpose_2019} and subsequently encoding the resulting joint graphs with spatial temporal graph convolutional networks (ST-GCN)~\cite{yan_stgcn_2018}, these methods achieve a degree of viewpoint and background invariance not attainable from raw pixel inputs. Graph neural network (GNN) variants applied to interaction-level violence detection~\cite{yun_missiongnn_2024} have reported competitive accuracy on controlled benchmarks. However, skeleton-based pipelines exhibit a critical dependency on reliable pose estimation: partial occlusion, low image resolution, or large inter-person distances---all common in real-world CCTV footage---frequently produce incomplete or erroneous skeleton sequences that degrade downstream classification. Furthermore, GNN architectures require explicit construction of a person-interaction graph, making them sensitive to tracking failures and computationally intensive when the number of persons in the scene varies dynamically.

To mitigate these limitations, recent work has explored multi-stage detection pipelines that interleave candidate region extraction, motion-based screening, and deep classification~\cite{yun_missiongnn_2024, nguyen_multistage_2021}. By discarding irrelevant background regions prior to deep inference, these cascaded architectures reduce computational overhead and suppress false alarms triggered by bystander motion. Entropy-based filtering is frequently employed as an intermediate screening step to reject non-violent activities that share superficial kinematic similarities with genuine violence.

More recently, Transformer-based architectures have emerged as a compelling alternative to convolutional and recurrent designs for video understanding~\cite{vaswani_attention_2023, dosovitskiy_image_2021, bertasius_is_2021, arnab_vivit_2021, pan_violence_2022}. Self-attention mechanisms enable Transformers to establish direct pairwise dependencies between any two temporal positions in a sequence, bypassing the sequential computation constraints of RNNs and the locality constraints of CNNs. Violence-specific attention-based models~\cite{pan_violence_2022} have demonstrated measurable gains over CNN--LSTM baselines. Nonetheless, vanilla video Transformers process full-resolution frames and thus incur quadratic complexity in the number of spatial tokens, making real-time inference on resource-constrained CCTV hardware impractical.

Hierarchical window-based architectures, particularly the Swin Transformer~\cite{9710580}, address this scalability concern by restricting self-attention to local non-overlapping windows and introducing a shifted-window mechanism to enable cross-window information exchange. This design achieves linear computational complexity with respect to image resolution while preserving strong representational power across multiple spatial scales. In the violence detection context, applying such a backbone exclusively to cropped interaction regions---rather than full frames---further amplifies its computational advantages and suppresses feature-level noise from irrelevant background regions.

Building on these observations, the present work advances the multi-stage paradigm by replacing the CNN--BiLSTM recognition core with a Transformer-based spatiotemporal encoder. Specifically, an ROI-based Swin Transformer provides hierarchical spatial encoding of candidate interaction regions, while a lightweight Temporal Transformer models clip-level behavioral evolution across frames. This design directly addresses the spatial context limitation and the long-range temporal dependency bottleneck identified in both skeleton-graph approaches and prior CNN--LSTM frameworks.


\section{Proposed Method}
To address the challenge of real-time violence detection, the primary difficulty lies not only in model accuracy but also in effectively filtering out noise from normal activities, which constitute the majority of surveillance footage. Therefore, rather than feeding raw data directly into resource-intensive deep neural networks, this study constructs a multi-stage screening mechanism whose overall architecture is depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:full_process}. The proposed framework comprises two principal stages: (i)~a dynamics-based preliminary screening stage that performs adaptive grouping of tracked individuals, extracts micro-physical kinematic features within a sliding temporal window, and applies an SVM pre-classifier to efficiently discard non-violent candidates; and (ii)~a deep recognition stage in which an ROI-based Swin Transformer encodes hierarchical spatial representations from candidate regions, followed by a Temporal Transformer that captures long-range temporal dependencies across frames, ultimately yielding a binary violence prediction through clip-level aggregation and MLP classification.

\subsection{High-risk Group Detection and Dynamics-based Preliminary Classification}

The first stage of the proposed framework is designed to efficiently identify and filter potential violent interactions from continuous surveillance streams before invoking computationally expensive deep learning models. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:high_risk_process}, this stage operates as a sequential pipeline comprising five key modules: (i)~multi-source data normalization, which standardizes heterogeneous input videos in both spatial and temporal domains; (ii)~real-time human detection and tracking using YOLOv8~\cite{jocher_yolov8_2023} and DeepSORT~\cite{wojke_simple_2017}; (iii)~adaptive proximity-based grouping that dynamically clusters individuals into interaction groups based on perspective-aware distance thresholds; (iv)~micro-physical and visual feature extraction within a sliding temporal window, producing a compact 7-dimensional descriptor that encodes kinematic intensity, impulse characteristics, directional chaos, and optical flow energy; and (v)~a two-tier filtering mechanism combining physics-based thresholding with an SVM pre-classifier to rapidly discard non-violent candidates. By performing early-stage elimination of the vast majority of normal activities, this module substantially reduces the computational burden on the subsequent deep recognition stage while preserving high recall for genuinely violent interactions.

\subsubsection{Multi-source Data Normalization and Perspective Adaptation}
Real-world surveillance data is often heterogeneous, characterized by significant variations in resolution and frame rate (FPS). Directly applying physical thresholds to raw data would lead to substantial discrepancies; for instance, the same hand gesture would result in a much larger pixel displacement in 4K video compared to SD video. To ensure consistency for physical calculations, we normalize all input frames to a fixed width of $W_{target} = 640\text{px}$. This empirical choice is based on the fact that 640px is the native input resolution of the YOLOv8 architecture~\cite{jocher_yolov8_2023}, optimizing human detection accuracy without inducing interpolation artifacts, while significantly reducing the computational cost for Optical Flow algorithms in subsequent steps. Furthermore, velocity and acceleration metrics are scaled by the source video's FPS to ensure temporal invariance.

Following object detection and tracking using YOLOv8~\cite{jocher_yolov8_2023} and DeepSORT~\cite{wojke_simple_2017}, a challenge arises regarding perspective distortion in 2D cameras, which renders interaction distance estimation inaccurate. Instead of using a fixed threshold, we employ an \textit{Adaptive Grouping} mechanism. The interaction threshold $T_{adaptive}$ between two individuals is dynamically calculated based on their own bounding box dimensions:

\begin{equation}
T_{adaptive} = K \times \frac{H_i + H_j}{2}
\label{eq:adaptive_threshold}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $H_i$ and $H_j$ are the heights of the bounding boxes of individuals $i$ and $j$, respectively. Height is selected as the scale proxy because, under perspective projection, a person at greater depth appears shorter in pixel space proportionally to their distance, making $H$ a more reliable and consistent indicator of real-world scale than width. This approach allows the system to self-adjust its sensitivity: the threshold is smaller for distant objects (small boxes) and larger for nearby objects, thereby more accurately reflecting the physical spatial relationship regardless of camera distance.

\subsubsection{Micro-physical Feature Extraction}
At each timestamp $t$, the system calculates instantaneous physical metrics for each individual in a group. A critical aspect of our method is the \textit{height normalization} technique. The instantaneous velocity $v_t$ is divided by the object's height ($H_t$), eliminating depth-induced errors and transforming pixel velocity into an index representing the relative energy consumption of the body:

\begin{equation}
v_t = \frac{\sqrt{(x_t - x_{t-1})^2 + (y_t - y_{t-1})^2}}{H_t} \times \text{FPS}
\label{eq:velocity}
\end{equation}

\noindent Instantaneous acceleration ($a_t$) and movement direction angle ($\theta_t$) are also computed to serve as the basis for the subsequent aggregation step.

\subsubsection{Visual Feature Extraction and Heatmap Representation}

In addition to analyzing bounding box coordinates, we extract visual features to capture subtle movements that the bounding box centroid may overlook, such as the rapid motion of limbs. We employ the Farneback Dense Optical Flow algorithm~\cite{farneback_2003} on two consecutive grayscale frames, $I_{t-1}$ and $I_{t}$. The conversion to grayscale is performed to reduce the computational workload and to focus the analysis on light intensity changes rather than chromatic information.

For each pixel $(x, y)$, the algorithm solves the displacement equations to determine the motion vector $V = (u, v)$, where $u$ and $v$ represent the horizontal and vertical displacement components, respectively. Subsequently, a motion energy representation, termed the "Heatmap," is constructed based on the magnitude of these vectors:

\begin{equation}
M(x, y) = \sqrt{u^2 + v^2}
\label{eq:heatmap_magnitude}
\end{equation}

The resulting heatmap $M(x, y)$ is a single-channel matrix where the brightness of each pixel is directly proportional to the movement speed at that specific location. Regions with high-intensity movements, such as punches or kicks, appear as bright, vibrant areas, whereas static objects result in black pixels ($M \approx 0$). This representation provides a localized, intuitive visualization of the motion intensity within the identified Region of Interest (ROI), serving as a crucial visual descriptor for distinguishing between normal and violent interactions. The use of optical flow as a complementary motion representation follows established practices in action recognition~\cite{simonyan_two-stream_2014} and violence detection~\cite{gracia_fast_2015}.

The effectiveness of combining physical and visual feature descriptors is concretely illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap_analysis}. In this frame, the system detects Group 1 consisting of two individuals (ID 2 and ID 3) engaged in physical altercation. From a visual perspective, the heatmap accurately reflects the energy distribution: static background regions (walls, vegetation) are completely suppressed (cold blue tones, $M \approx 0$), while the conflict zone concentrates maximum energy (bright red). This results in a heatmap variance index ($\sigma_{heat}$) of $2.96$, indicating pronounced contrast between subjects and background.

More importantly, analysis of the 7-dimensional feature vector $V_{raw}$ reveals strong alignment with the impulse and chaos hypotheses. Although the group's mean velocity is relatively high ($\mu_v \approx 69.49$), the decisive indicator is the Maximum Acceleration ($A_{max}$) reaching $74.42$ — far exceeding the safety threshold of $15.0$. This value represents the abrupt velocity change (jerk) characteristic of punches or forceful impacts. Simultaneously, the Interaction Entropy ($S_{interaction}$) registers $103.03^\circ$, evidencing that the two subjects are moving in opposing or chaotic directions, distinctly different from the parallel motion of pedestrians walking together. The combination of these indicators triggers a Violence Risk alert, enabling the system to accurately localize the event for subsequent processing.

\subsubsection{Feature Vector Aggregation and Dynamic Analysis}
Violent behavior rarely occurs as a single isolated frame but rather as a sequence of continuous fluctuations. Therefore, we establish a sliding window of size $N=10$ frames. From the raw data collected within this window, we perform statistical aggregation to compress temporal information into a fixed spatial representation. The result is a 7-dimensional feature vector, denoted as $V_{raw}$, defined as follows:

\begin{equation}
V_{raw} = [\mu_v, \sigma_v, \mu_a, A_{max}, S_{interaction}, \mu_{heat}, \sigma_{heat}]
\label{eq:feature_vector}
\end{equation}

\noindent Each component in this vector is designed to capture a specific physical aspect of the behavior:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Kinetic Energy Features ($\mu_v, \sigma_v$):} We utilize the mean ($\mu_v$) and standard deviation ($\sigma_v$) of the normalized velocity. Physically, $\mu_v$ represents the average energy consumption; a stationary group will have $\mu_v \approx 0$. However, to distinguish between jogging and fighting, $\sigma_v$ is employed to measure motion stability. Violent behaviors typically exhibit "jerky" characteristics (sudden lunges followed by pauses), resulting in a significantly higher $\sigma_v$ compared to harmonic motion.

    \item \textbf{Impulse Features ($\mu_a, A_{max}$):} This is a key contribution of the proposed method. Based on Newton's Second Law, the impact force is proportional to acceleration. We place particular emphasis on \textit{Maximum Acceleration} ($A_{max}$) within the sliding window rather than solely relying on the mean. The rationale is that punches or kicks often occur very rapidly, creating a massive acceleration "spike" that the mean value might obscure. $A_{max}$ serves as a biometric signature for detecting high-intensity impacts.

    \item \textbf{Directional Chaos Feature ($S_{interaction}$):} To differentiate a chaotic crowd from an orderly moving group, we employ Interaction Entropy, calculated via the standard deviation of movement angles ($\sigma_{\theta}$). In non-violent scenarios, velocity vectors tend to be parallel or cohesive (low $\sigma_{\theta}$). Conversely, brawls are characterized by random directional dispersion: attackers lunging, victims recoiling or falling, causing the Entropy value to surge.

    \item \textbf{Visual Energy Features ($\mu_{heat}, \sigma_{heat}$):} Parallel to the bounding box analysis, an Optical Flow Heatmap is generated. $\mu_{heat}$ measures the total motion intensity across the entire region of interest, helping to detect subtle limb movements that the bounding box might miss. Meanwhile, $\sigma_{heat}$ measures spatial distribution; a fast punch creates a localized bright spot on a dark background, leading to a high standard deviation.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Filtering Mechanism and Early Classification}

To optimize real-time performance, we design a two-step filtering process applied to each temporal window sample---that is, each $V_{raw}$ vector aggregated from the most recent 10-frame sliding window of a given interaction group. The first step is a physics-based thresholding filter that immediately discards samples exhibiting low motion intensity. A window sample is retained as a violence candidate only if it satisfies the impulse condition ($A_{max} > 15.0$) or the visual energy condition ($\mu_{heat} > 2.0$). These thresholds are derived from empirical observations that routine daily activities---walking, standing, or casual gesturing---rarely produce a velocity variation exceeding 15\% of body height within a sub-second interval. On our training set, this step alone eliminates approximately 60\%
of all temporal window samples, most of which correspond to stationary or low-motion situations.

The $V_{raw}$ vectors that survive the physics filter are subsequently normalized before classification. Because the components of $V_{raw}$ span vastly different scales---$A_{max}$ can reach several hundred while $\sigma_{heat}$ typically fluctuates between 0 and 5---we apply zero-mean unit-variance normalization (StandardScaler) independently to each feature dimension. This step is critical for the downstream SVM with an RBF kernel, whose decision boundary depends on Euclidean distances between data points; without normalization, high-magnitude features would dominate the classification outcome.

To train the SVM pre-classifier, we construct a dedicated training set from Stage~A pipeline outputs. Specifically, the complete set of training videos (both violent and non-violent) is processed through YOLOv8~\cite{jocher_yolov8_2023} detection, DeepSORT~\cite{wojke_simple_2017} tracking, adaptive grouping, and micro-physical feature extraction. This process yields 4{,}250 
seven-dimensional $V_{raw}$ samples, each corresponding to a single 10-frame sliding window of a particular interaction group at a specific time instant in the video stream. Labels are inherited from the source video: a sample is labeled positive (\textit{violent}) if the source video is annotated as violent and the group under consideration is involved in the violent interaction during the corresponding time span; all remaining samples are labeled negative (\textit{non-violent}). To verify labeling quality, we manually inspect a random subset of 500 
samples and confirm a labeling accuracy of 96.8\%.
The final SVM dataset comprises 1{,}870 
positive and 2{,}380 
negative samples, reflecting the natural class imbalance in surveillance footage where the majority of temporal windows correspond to normal activity. This dataset is partitioned using a stratified 80/20 split, with 80\% allocated to training and hyperparameter optimization and 20\% reserved for independent evaluation.

The normalized vectors are classified by a Support Vector Machine (SVM) with a Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel, defined as:
\begin{equation}
K(x_i, x_j) = \exp\bigl(-\gamma \|x_i - x_j\|^2\bigr)
\label{eq:rbf_kernel}
\end{equation}

\noindent SVM-RBF~\cite{cortes_svm_1995} is selected for this pre-classification step for three reasons. First, the feature space has only seven dimensions, which is compact enough for SVM to generalize well without requiring the large-scale data that deep learning models typically demand. Second, the RBF kernel induces a nonlinear decision boundary, which is appropriate given the inherently nonlinear distribution of violent versus non-violent behaviors in the physical feature space. Third, SVM inference is computationally negligible---requiring only distance computations to the support vectors---and therefore introduces no meaningful latency into the real-time pipeline. The two key hyperparameters---the penalty coefficient $C$ and the kernel parameter $\gamma$---are optimized via grid search combined with 5-fold stratified cross-validation on the training partition. The search space covers $C \in \{0.1, 1, 10, 100\}$ and $\gamma \in \{\texttt{scale}, \texttt{auto}, 0.01, 0.1, 1\}$. The optimal configuration is found at $C = 10$
and $\gamma = \texttt{scale}$, 
achieving a cross-validation accuracy of 74.8\%.

It is important to emphasize that the SVM at this stage serves as a \textit{gatekeeper} rather than the final classifier. Its operating point is deliberately calibrated to favor recall over precision: tolerating additional non-violent false positives is preferable to missing a genuinely violent window, because any sample rejected by the SVM is permanently excluded from Stage~B consideration and cannot be recovered. To enforce this policy, the SVM decision threshold is adjusted so that violent recall on the validation set reaches at least 77.3\%,
accepting a precision of 60.5\% 
at this stage---the moderate precision is by design, as the remaining false positives are subsequently handled by Stage~B (Swin + Temporal Transformer). Table~\ref{tab:svm_performance} summarizes the per-class breakdown. The limited discriminative power of a 7-dimensional handcrafted descriptor is expected; the SVM is not intended to replace a deep recognizer but rather to reduce the candidate pool to a manageable size. Overall, the combination of physics-based thresholding and SVM filtering eliminates approximately 67\% 
of all temporal window samples, forwarding roughly 33\% 
of the candidates to Stage~B for deep analysis. At that point, the system extracts a $T = 16$ consecutive-frame ROI sequence centered on the flagged time instant, where the Swin--Temporal Transformer provides the fine-grained spatiotemporal reasoning that the compact physical features cannot achieve.

To quantify Stage~A's impact at the video level---the metric most operationally relevant for deployment---we analyze whether each violent source video generates at least one forwarded window to Stage~B. On the training partition, Stage~A successfully forwards at least one candidate window for \textbf{96.2\%} of all violent source videos, confirming that fewer than 4\% of violent events are permanently suppressed before reaching the deep recognizer. The remaining 3.8\% correspond predominantly to slow-escalation scenarios in which neither $A_{max}$ nor $\mu_{heat}$ exceeds the physics-based thresholds across the entire 10-frame sliding window---a failure mode explicitly acknowledged in the Limitations section.

\subsection{ROI-based Swin–Temporal Transformer}
While the dynamics-based filter in Stage~A effectively reduces the candidate pool by rejecting interactions with insufficient kinematic evidence, it cannot distinguish between genuinely violent events and confounding activities that share similar motion signatures---such as abrupt physical contact in sports, animated celebrations, or forceful greetings. These ambiguous cases require a spatial and temporal understanding of body posture, relative movement, and interaction dynamics that handcrafted kinematic descriptors fundamentally cannot provide. Stage~B addresses this by applying an ROI-based Swin Transformer~\cite{9710580} for hierarchical spatial encoding of candidate regions, coupled with a Temporal Transformer encoder for clip-level violence recognition.

\subsubsection{Swin Transformer for ROI Spatial Feature Extraction}

Violent interactions in surveillance footage are characterized by subtle, spatially compact patterns of physical contact---rapid limb movements, forceful body displacements, and close-range interpersonal dynamics---that are easily occluded by neighboring persons or obscured by cluttered backgrounds. Modeling these cues demands a spatial encoder capable of resolving fine-grained local structure while simultaneously capturing contextual relationships across the entire interaction region.

The Swin Transformer~\cite{9710580} satisfies both requirements through its hierarchical, window-based self-attention design. Each ROI frame $I_t \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 3}$ is partitioned into non-overlapping patches, which are projected to embedding vectors via a linear layer. The backbone comprises four stages; at each stage, patch merging progressively halves the spatial resolution while doubling the channel dimension, constructing a multi-scale feature hierarchy. Within each stage, Transformer blocks alternate between window-based multi-head self-attention (W-MSA), which captures intra-window structure at low computational cost, and shifted window-based multi-head self-attention (SW-MSA), which establishes cross-window information exchange. The early stages of this hierarchy retain high-resolution spatial detail---critical for resolving joint-level interactions such as hand contact or arm extension---while deeper stages aggregate these local cues into semantically rich representations of body posture and group configuration.

The output of the Swin backbone for each ROI frame $I_t$ is a spatial feature map $F_t$, which is condensed into a single frame-level descriptor via global average pooling (GAP):
\begin{equation}
F_t = f_{\text{swin}}(I_t), \quad z_t = \text{GAP}(F_t) \in \mathbb{R}^d
\end{equation}

\noindent To standardize the descriptor dimension across backbone variants, $z_t$ is passed through a linear projection layer:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{z}_t = Wz_t + b, \quad \tilde{z}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_0}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $d_0 = 768$ in our implementation, matching the hidden dimension of the Temporal Transformer encoder.

Restricting Swin encoding to Stage~A-filtered ROIs rather than full video frames yields two compounding benefits. First, the reduction in spatial extent directly decreases the number of patch tokens processed per forward pass, which is critical for maintaining throughput in a continuous CCTV stream. Second, and more importantly, confining attention computation to the interaction region suppresses interference from irrelevant background motion---passersby, vegetation, traffic---that would otherwise dilute the feature representation and increase the false-positive rate in downstream classification.


\subsubsection{Temporal Transformer for Clip-level Violence Modeling}

Violence is a temporally structured phenomenon: a complete violent event typically progresses through a recognizable sequence of phases---approach, physical contact, and recoil---each of which may individually resemble non-violent motion when examined in isolation. Frame-level classifiers are inherently blind to such sequential structure, and recurrent architectures such as LSTM are susceptible to cumulative hidden-state degradation over longer clips. The Temporal Transformer encoder addresses these limitations by modeling behavioral dynamics directly in the clip-level feature space through global self-attention over the temporal axis.

For each Stage~A-filtered ROI, a clip of $T$ uniformly sampled frames is constructed:

\begin{equation}
\{I_t\}_{t=1}^{T}
\label{eq:clip_frames}
\end{equation}

\noindent We set $T = 16$, a choice that balances temporal context coverage against per-clip inference latency. To extend the effective observation window without increasing the token count, frames are sampled at a stride of $s$ frames, where $s$ is determined by the source frame rate.

During training, stochastic temporal augmentations are applied to promote invariance to absolute clip position: random clip start offset, small temporal jitter on the sampling stride, and probabilistic frame dropout. These augmentations are applied independently per clip and do not alter the number of tokens consumed by the Temporal Transformer.

Using the frame-level descriptor $\tilde{z}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_0}$ produced by the Swin encoder for each frame, the clip is represented as a matrix:

\begin{equation}
Z = [\tilde{z}_1, \tilde{z}_2, \ldots, \tilde{z}_T] \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_0}
\label{eq:clip_matrix}
\end{equation}

\noindent Since self-attention is permutation-invariant, temporal order is injected by adding a learnable positional encoding $P \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_0}$ to the token sequence:

\begin{equation}
H^0 = Z + P
\label{eq:positional_encoding}
\end{equation}

\noindent The input $H^0$ is then processed by $L$ successive Transformer encoder layers, each comprising multi-head self-attention (MHSA) over the temporal axis followed by a position-wise feed-forward sublayer and residual connections:

\begin{equation}
H^\ell = \text{TransformerEnc}(H^{\ell-1}), \quad \ell = 1, \ldots, L
\label{eq:transformer_layer}
\end{equation}

\noindent In our configuration, $L = 2$ encoder layers are used, each with $h = 8$ attention heads operating on the full $d_0 = 768$-dimensional space (head dimension $d_k = 96$). A dropout rate of $p = 0.1$ is applied to both the attention weights and the feed-forward activations within each layer to mitigate overfitting on the relatively compact ROI clip dataset.

Self-attention computes pairwise compatibility scores between every pair of temporal positions $(t_i, t_j)$, enabling the encoder to selectively integrate evidence from the approach phase, the moment of contact, and the recoil phase simultaneously. This global receptive field over the clip is the primary architectural advantage over LSTM, which must propagate information sequentially and is prone to gradient attenuation across long time spans.

After $L$ encoder layers, the clip representation $H^L \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_0}$ is aggregated via temporal mean pooling:

\begin{equation}
g = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} H^L[t] \in \mathbb{R}^{d_0}
\label{eq:temporal_pooling}
\end{equation}

\noindent Mean pooling assigns equal contribution to all frames, which is appropriate given that violent actions may appear at any position within the sampled clip rather than being concentrated at fixed temporal locations.

The pooled vector $g$ is passed through a two-layer MLP with a hidden dimension of $d_0 / 2 = 384$ and ReLU activation, followed by a single linear output unit. The model is optimized end-to-end with binary cross-entropy with logits loss (\texttt{BCEWithLogitsLoss}), which numerically combines the sigmoid activation and binary cross-entropy into a single stable operation:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = -\bigl[y \log \sigma(\hat{y}) + (1 - y) \log (1 - \sigma(\hat{y}))\bigr]
\label{eq:bce_loss}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\hat{y}$ is the raw logit output of the MLP and $y \in \{0, 1\}$ is the ground-truth violence label.

The temporal self-attention mechanism confers two concrete operational benefits over frame-level or recurrent classifiers. First, by jointly attending to all frames, the encoder can identify approach--contact--recoil transitions whose decisive signal is distributed across multiple non-adjacent frames---a structure that LSTM hidden states may fail to preserve intact over 16 time steps. Second, the absence of sequential computation removes the dependency on error-free tracking continuity: a missed detection on one or two frames within the clip does not corrupt the representation for subsequent frames, as each token is independently encoded before attention is applied.

\subsubsection{Integrating Swin and Temporal Transformer in the Multi-stage Pipeline}

Stage~B operates exclusively on the subset of ROIs forwarded by Stage~A, thereby avoiding full-frame processing and the associated computational overhead. Within Stage~B, the Swin backbone provides spatial encoding for each ROI frame, extracting hierarchical descriptors that capture both fine-grained interaction details and broader body-level context. The Temporal Transformer then aggregates these per-frame descriptors across the clip, modeling the temporal progression of the interaction to produce a single clip-level representation for binary violence classification.

A key design principle of this architecture is the explicit decoupling of spatial representation learning from temporal dependency modeling. The Swin backbone is pretrained on large-scale image data~\cite{deng_imagenet_2009} and fine-tuned as a feature extractor, while the Temporal Transformer is trained from scratch on the ROI clip dataset. This separation allows each module to specialize in its respective objective and simplifies the optimization landscape relative to jointly trained 3D convolutional architectures.

The cascade design delivers two compounding operational benefits. First, because Stage~B processes only the filtered ROI subset, average GPU utilization per video is substantially lower than an unconstrained deep recognizer applied to all detected person-group crops. Second, the spatial gating performed by Stage~A delivers a cleaner input distribution to Stage~B: candidate clips have already passed a kinematic plausibility check, reducing the prevalence of easily-dismissed non-violent samples in the deep classifier's input and concentrating Stage~B capacity on genuinely ambiguous cases.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All experiments were conducted on a workstation equipped with an \textit{NVIDIA GeForce RTX 2080 Ti} GPU featuring 11~GB of video memory and 32~GB of system RAM, running Windows 11 Pro 64-bit. The CPU is an \textit{Intel Core i9-10900K}. This hardware configuration represents a realistic deployment scenario for professional CCTV systems, where real-time processing is a critical requirement.

The deep recognition stage (Stage~B) was trained using the ROI-based clip dataset generated after the multi-stage filtering process. 
Each training sample consists of a sequence of 16 consecutive ROI frames resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels and normalized using ImageNet statistics. 
Temporal clips were sampled uniformly to preserve short-term behavioral evolution while maintaining computational efficiency.

The spatial encoder is based on the Swin Tiny architecture~\cite{9710580} (\texttt{swin\_tiny\_patch4\_window7\_224}), pretrained on ImageNet-1K~\cite{deng_imagenet_2009}. 
For each frame, the Swin backbone produces a 768-dimensional feature representation. 
These frame-level descriptors are then fed into a Temporal Transformer encoder composed of 2 layers with 8 attention heads and a hidden dimension of 768. 
A learnable positional encoding is applied to preserve temporal order. 
The final clip-level representation is obtained via temporal mean pooling, followed by a linear classification layer for binary prediction.
The complete model contains 41,709,691 trainable parameters.

Model optimization was performed with AdamW~\cite{loshchilov_adamw_2019} using an initial learning rate of $1\times10^{-4}$ and a weight decay coefficient of $0.01$. The learning rate followed a cosine annealing schedule (\texttt{CosineAnnealingLR}) with a minimum value of $1\times10^{-6}$ over 50 training epochs. Training was conducted with a batch size of 8 and \texttt{BCEWithLogitsLoss} as the binary classification objective. In addition, automatic mixed precision (AMP) was employed to improve computational efficiency and stabilize GPU memory usage during training.

\subsection{Quantitative Results}

We evaluate the complete two-stage pipeline on all 1149 held-out test clips. The full framework achieves 91.8\% overall accuracy, 89.7\% precision, 93.1\% recall, and a 91.4\% F1-score. The AUC-ROC reaches 92.04\% as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:evaluation_metrics}(a), and the AUC-PR is 89.53\%, both reflecting reliable class separation across a broad range of operating thresholds.

Fig.~\ref{fig:evaluation_metrics}(b) details the per-class breakdown. Of 574 non-violent test clips, 513 were correctly rejected (TN = 89.4\%) and 61 generated false alarms (FP = 10.6\%). For the 575 violent clips, 535 were correctly detected (TP = 93.0\%) while 40 were missed (FN = 7.0\%). The low false-negative count is partly attributable to the Temporal Transformer modeling short approach--contact--recoil sequences over the 16-frame window, patterns that frame-level or recurrent classifiers miss when the decisive action spans only a few frames. Remaining false positives arise from hard-negative segments---crowd surges, contact sports, and close-range physical greetings---where kinematic signatures temporarily resemble those of genuine violence and Stage~A conservatively forwards the clip to Stage~B.

Stage~A alone achieves 82.4\% recall at 76.1\% precision. The gap is largely expected: momentum and directional entropy capture high-energy interactions well but cannot separate genuine violence from contact sports or aggressive play that share similar physical signatures. The SVM operates in a compact feature space with no spatial or contextual awareness, so it reaches a natural ceiling in visually complex scenes.

Adding per-frame Swin encoding without temporal context (\textit{Swin Only}) raises accuracy to 87.19\%. Spatial features resolve many per-frame ambiguities, but clips where violence develops gradually---through a buildup-and-escalation pattern no single frame cleanly captures---remain problematic. The complete two-stage pipeline reaches 91.8\% accuracy and a 91.4\% F1-score, a 2.3 percentage-point gain in F1 over Stage~B alone. Stage~A improves input quality by removing most non-violent candidates before deep inference runs, while Stage~B handles the ambiguous cases that kinematic features cannot resolve on their own. Neither module reproduces this performance independently, and the gain is achieved without a proportional increase in computational cost because Stage~B only fires on the reduced filtered set.

CNN-LSTM~\cite{patel_realtime_2021} scores 82.4\% accuracy. The attention-based network~\cite{pan_violence_2022} mitigates some of this by selectively weighting regions, reaching 87.9\%, but it still operates on full frames and must allocate capacity to bystanders and static background.

ROIs reaching Stage~B have already passed a kinematic plausibility check, resulting in a substantially cleaner and more focused input. This gating step reduces the feature-level confusion that full-frame models encounter and largely explains the performance gap observed in Table~\ref{tab:sota}.

The framework achieves 93.1\% recall with precision held at 89.7\%. In security deployments, the cost of a missed violent event generally outweighs that of a spurious alert an operator can dismiss in seconds, and our calibration reflects this priority. The precision level remains above the range where persistent false alarms would create meaningful alert fatigue in practice.

In terms of throughput, Stage~B averages 41.72~ms per clip on the RTX~2080~Ti, roughly 24~FPS, which is competitive with the tested baselines and adequate for real-time deployment. Because Stage~B is bypassed entirely for candidates rejected by Stage~A, actual system throughput over a continuous feed is higher than the isolated Stage~B figure implies.


\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

This paper proposes a multi-stage violence detection framework for real-time CCTV surveillance systems, addressing limitations of existing methods in handling complex situations such as crowded scenes, small-scale violent interactions, and fast movements that visually resemble aggression. The principal contributions are threefold: (1) a dynamics-based preliminary screening mechanism employing adaptive proximity grouping and a 7-dimensional micro-physical feature descriptor with SVM pre-classification, which efficiently suppresses non-violent candidates before invoking deep inference; (2) an ROI-based Swin Transformer spatial encoder that concentrates representational capacity on high-risk interaction regions, eliminating background interference that degrades full-frame models; and (3) a Temporal Transformer encoder that models behavioral evolution across a 16-frame clip through global self-attention, overcoming the sequential bottleneck and long-range dependency limitations of recurrent architectures. Evaluated on a unified benchmark of 1,149 held-out clips drawn from six heterogeneous sources, the complete pipeline achieves 91.8\% accuracy, 91.4\% F1-score, and 92.04\% AUC-ROC, while sustaining real-time throughput at 24~FPS on a single mid-range GPU.

\section*{Limitations.} 
Despite these results, the framework operates under several structural constraints that practitioners should consider before deployment. First, the multi-person interaction paradigm underlying Stage~A becomes ineffective in two edge cases: (i) \textit{complete mutual occlusion}, where tracked bounding boxes collapse or merge under dense crowd pressure, preventing reliable centroid estimation and rendering adaptive grouping unreliable; and (ii) \textit{single-person violence}, such as self-harm or weapon-based attacks directed at non-present targets, where no interaction pair is formed and Stage~A produces no candidate group to forward to Stage~B. Second, the SVM gatekeeper in Stage~A is calibrated to maximize recall at the cost of moderate precision, yet it can still miss violent events whose kinematic signature falls below the physics-based thresholds---most notably slow-escalation assaults where peak acceleration remains subdued across the 10-frame sliding window, or restraint scenarios in which the aggressor's movement is deliberately controlled to avoid triggering motion-energy filters. Any sample rejected at this stage is permanently excluded from Stage~B consideration and cannot be recovered by the deep recognizer. Third, and most foundationally, the entire pipeline inherits the detection quality of the YOLOv8 person detector~\cite{jocher_yolov8_2023}. Missed detections in low-illumination environments, heavy rain artifacts, extreme fisheye distortion near frame boundaries, or extreme scale variation between distant and proximate subjects directly reduce the number of valid tracks available for grouping, potentially causing genuine violent interactions to be silently dropped before any feature analysis is performed. Mitigating this dependency through detector ensembling or domain-adaptive fine-tuning on target camera profiles represents a necessary step toward robust operational deployment.

\section*{Future Work.} 
Three directions are identified for subsequent research. First, the spatial encoder could be upgraded to Swin Transformer V2~\cite{liu_swin_2022} or a lightweight mobile variant to lower per-clip latency and extend applicability to embedded CCTV hardware; simultaneously, incorporating synchronized audio streams through a cross-modal attention mechanism may resolve visually ambiguous cases that neither kinematic nor appearance features can discriminate reliably. Second, the binary classification objective could be extended to a multi-class formulation distinguishing assault, robbery, weapon display, and crowd stampede, coupled with a severity regression head to enable graduated alert escalation and reduce operator fatigue caused by uniform alarm treatment. Third, the dependence on YOLOv8 detection quality motivates investigation of detector-agnostic grouping strategies---for example, keypoint-based proximity estimation that remains robust under partial occlusion---and long-horizon pilot deployments in live environments, including schools and transit hubs, to gather deployment feedback and assess algorithmic bias across demographic groups under realistic operational conditions.
